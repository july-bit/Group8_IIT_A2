What is Robots?

According to the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), cloud computing is a model for enabling ubiquitous, convenient, on-demand network access to a shared pool of configurable computing resources (e.g., networks, servers, storage, applications, and services) that can be rapidly provisioned and released with minimal management effort or service provider interaction. (Ruparelia 2016, p. 4).
That means, rather than having to spend money for purchasing and maintaining costly IT infrastructures, businesses can now rent access to anything from applications, storage, networking, databases, etc. from a cloud service provider (CSP) such as Amazon Web Services (AWS), Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, etc (Ranger 2018).
Cloud computing generally follows three delivery models, namely:

  • Public clouds: These are owned and operated by 3rd-party CSPs, of which all hardware, software, and other supporting infrastructure is owned and managed by the cloud provider. This model is what most people are familiar with, where the consumption of public cloud services (files storage on Google Drive, music streaming platforms, Office Online, etc.) happens via the Internet rather than a private or restricted network (Ruparelia 2016, p. 30).
  • Private clouds: Here, computing resources are used exclusively by a single business, organization, or in some cases, a single person. A private cloud can be physically located on the company’s on-site datacenter, allowing the company to control how the data is being used, shared, stored, as well as how to build the infrastructure they want. This model is particularly useful for organizations that prioritise security and privacy of their data above all else.
  • Hybrid clouds: A combination of public and private clouds, bound together by technology that allows data and applications to be shared between them. By allowing data and applications to move between private and public clouds, a hybrid cloud gives businesses greater flexibility in terms of daily operations.

    • Within these models, there exists three types of cloud services, or “stacks”, as they are built on top of one another:

  • Infrastructure as a service (IaaS): The most basic category of cloud computing service or the base stack, where companies can rent out IT infrastructures such as physical or virtual servicers, storage, network on a pay-as-you-go basis.
  • Platform as a service (PaaS): The next layer up where CPSs provide an on-demand environment for developing, testing, delivering and managing software applications. Thus, making it easier for developers to quickly create web or mobile apps, without having to worry about the underlying infrastructure needed for development work.
  • Software as a service (SaaS): The most common form of cloud computing where applications are delivered over the internet, on demand and typically on a subscription basis e.g. Google G Suite, Microsoft 365, Adobe Creative Cloud, etc. With SaaS, cloud providers host and manage the software application and underlying infrastructure. Users connect to the application over the Internet usually via a web browser or on their phone or tablet. Ranger (2018, para. 18) noted that SaaS spending in 2019 was dominated by application purchases, making up for more than half of all public cloud spending.

    • What is the likely impact?

    Within the workplace, Ruparelia (2016) predicted two major trends. The first trend is where workstations would gradually be replaced by zero or thin clients - machines that do not have applications installed on them- where workers would perform most daily tasks via cloud-based application via a web browser e.g. document-editing or emailing. This reduces the need for organizations to have to manage all those applications installed on a variety of workstation. Another benefit is data security since these thin clients would not contain any local disk but instead store data on the cloud (p.63).
    The second trend is ubiquitous computing or bring your own device (BYOD), where employees can access corporate-provided applications and information on their own personal devices anytime and anywhere. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated this trend by the fact that many employees were forced to work from home, often with their own devices. In the future, as more companies adopt the hybrid work model (where employees can split their time between working from home and at the office), it is possible to assume that this will result in cost reduction for company's IT department in term of It equipment deployment, maintenance, and so on.

For example, an entertainment cloud such as Apple TV, Google TV or Roku, where they can get access to their own library of on-demand and downloadable content.
